# GAME DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Shared creative principles across the studio. Individual games may adapt these in their own doctrine files (e.g., `overflow/supporting-doctrine/`).

## Core Player Needs (Self-Determination Theory)
Every design decision should serve at least one:
- **Autonomy** — the player feels they chose their path
- **Competence** — the player feels skilled and capable
- **Relatedness** — the player feels connected to the world or characters

## Additional Design Drivers
- **Curiosity** — reward exploration and discovery, not just execution
- **Meaningful decisions** — choices should have visible consequences
- **Authored trust** — teach the player the rules before punishing mistakes

## First-Session Philosophy
The first session is a promise. It must:
- teach mechanics through play, not walls of text
- build trust that the game is fair
- demonstrate that the world has depth worth exploring
- end with a hook that makes the player want to return

## Feel Over Features
A game that feels good with 3 features beats a game that feels bad with 30. Prioritize:
- responsive controls
- readable feedback (text, animations, sound)
- predictable physics
- satisfying impact on enemy hits and boss encounters

## Engagement Systems (when ready)
Systems that create long-term pull:
- **Progression** — visible growth (stats, unlocks, achievements)
- **Rivalry** — memorable recurring enemies or challenges
- **Legacy** — consequences that carry across runs
- **Narrative emergence** — unexpected stories from systems interacting
- **Community** — shareable moments (speedruns, screenshots, scores)

## What To Protect
- The opener is a promise — never break it
- The ending is sacred — never cheapen it
- Weirdness must pay rent — every oddity should serve gameplay or narrative
- Never punish the player for exploring
